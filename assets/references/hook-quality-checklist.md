# Hook Quality Checklist

This document covers everything about evaluating and creating hooks in the Genesis system: the quality checklist, the 10 power elements, the emotional viciousness scale, and pre-hook ideation techniques.

The hook is the highest-leverage line in the whole ad — **spend ~80% of your writing time on the hook** (and what immediately follows). The rest is structure the bots are good at.

---

## Hooks must be EMOTIONALLY VICIOUS

Not mean — **vicious.** A vicious hook grabs something the reader already feels (a fear, a shame, a dread, a suspicion) and **squeezes it so hard they physically cannot scroll past.** It goes right for the throat in the *very first line* — no throat-clearing, no warm-up. A polite, comfortable, hedged, or vague hook is a **dead hook.** If it doesn't make someone flinch, gasp, or feel personally called out, it's not vicious enough. **There is always a more vicious version** — go find the one that flinches.

**Every word must earn its place — protect the charged word.** "Sexless" can't become "unfulfilling"; "killing" can't become "hurting." If you reach for a softer, safer, clinical synonym, STOP — that instinct is the enemy. Push to the **edge of reality** (almost unbelievable, without crossing it). This is more art than science, built by saturating your brain on ads.

### The 8 principles
1. **Relevance in the first line** (the first 10–20 words). Three ways in: **pain/condition call-out** · **belief call-out** ("they told you red meat would clog your arteries") · **general openness** (broad intrigue — the hardest, biggest bets).
2. **Emotionally charged language** — visceral, embodied: *banned, sexless, stink, lying, die, naked, killing, terrifying, beg.*
3. **Natural intrigue / open loops** — promise a story or reveal the reader *has* to resolve.
4. **Stakes / rubbernecking** — real, present consequences they can't look away from.
5. **Specificity & tangibility** — "a 300-page report from 1910," not "an old report." Specific reads like a fact; vague reads like an ad.
6. **Doesn't read like an ad** — reads like news, a story, a confession, or an insight; the payoff is catharsis/intrigue, not a pitch.
7. **Caveman / Germanic language** — short, blunt, 5-year-old-simple. The French perspire, the Germans sweat — use the German word.
8. **Consequence first, mechanism later** — lead with the wound, not the scalpel. ("THIS habit spikes cortisol" is mechanism — invisible to the reader. Lead with what happens to *them*.)

## Hook Quality Checklist

Every hook must hit ALL of these:

- ☐ **10-20 words before the fold** — This is the text that appears before "See more" on Facebook
- ☐ **Tangible and specific** — Symptoms, numbers, concrete details (not abstractions)
- ☐ **Creates unresolved curiosity** — Reader MUST know what comes next
- ☐ **Relevant to audience pain + things they already know** — Speaks to their world
- ☐ **Promises value** — Insight, reframe, new information
- ☐ **Subtle sensationalism, not clickbait** — Bold but credible
- ☐ **Emotionally charged / visceral / visual language** — Physical, embodied words
- ☐ **Widely relevant** — Doesn't disqualify too early with niche specifics
- ☐ **Simple enough to understand immediately** — Not overly long or hard to digest
- ☐ **Reads like information/news, NOT like an ad** — Editorial tone
- ☐ **Level 3 emotional viciousness** — Emotional bomb, not vague or hinting

## The 10 Power Elements

Every hook should draw from as many of these as possible:

1. **Novel** — Something they haven't seen before
2. **Shocking** — Disrupts expectations
3. **Engaging** — Demands continued reading
4. **Controversial** — Challenges accepted wisdom
5. **Specific** — Numbers, names, details
6. **Unpredictable** — Doesn't go where they expect
7. **Bold** — Makes a strong claim
8. **Emotional** — Triggers a feeling, not just a thought
9. **Urgent** — Creates time pressure or consequence
10. **Topic-framed** — Clearly about their world

## Pre-Hook Ideation

Before generating hooks mechanically, extract SEEDS from the winning ads and brief:

### Step 1: Mine the Material
Read the brief and ask: what are ALL the interesting ideas, standout lines, sentiments, mechanisms? Don't just look at the obvious angle — look at everything buried in the material.

### Step 2: Promotion Check
For each seed, ask: how could I PROMOTE this idea into the main hook? A hook seed can come from ANYWHERE — body copy, mechanism, proof point, comment language, a single powerful phrase.

### Step 3: Apply These Three Techniques

#### Transfers
If the hook uses one concept, ask what CATEGORY that concept belongs to, then substitute.

Example: If the hook uses coffee, coffee is a RITUAL. What other rituals could replace it?
- Gym routine
- Sex
- Morning routine
- Alcohol tolerance
- Bedtime routine
- Prayer/meditation

The transfer preserves the emotional structure but creates a fresh hook.

#### Reframes
"It's not X, it's Y" — take the audience's existing belief about the cause and flip it.

Examples:
- "It's not low T, it's too high cortisol"
- "It's not your willpower, it's your gut bacteria"
- "It's not your age, it's your inflammation"
- "It's not genetics, it's your sleep architecture"

The reframe installs a new mechanism belief while invalidating the old one.

#### Promotions
Take a line buried in body copy and make it the opening. The most powerful ideas are often hidden in paragraph 3 or 4 of an existing ad, not in the hook.

Process:
1. Read through winning ad body copy
2. Highlight any line that creates a physical reaction (flinch, curiosity, discomfort)
3. Ask: what if THIS was the opening line?
4. Build the rest of the ad from that starting point

## Hook Relevance Rules

Hooks need to be relevant on two dimensions:

### 1. Pain Point (or desire)
The hook must clearly touch the audience's core concern. If the market is health-focused, the hook must signal "this is about your health" within the first few words.

### 2. Things They Know
The hook must reference things already in their awareness — symptoms, people, experiences, cultural touchstones.

Examples of leveraging what they know:
- "Benjamin Franklin used it. President Roosevelt relied on it." (known figures)
- "Do you wake up at 3 a.m. having to pee?" (known symptom)
- "Your granddad smoked a pack of cigs a day, washed it down with whiskey and still lived to 100." (known cultural observation)

Sometimes the relevance is explicit ("Are you not hungry for breakfast in the morning? That's high cortisol.") and sometimes implicit ("A single 300-page report published in 1910..."). Both work. The audience must recognize the topic is FOR THEM within seconds.

## Examples of Good Hooks (For Style Reference)

These demonstrate the qualities above — tangible, specific, creating curiosity, emotionally charged, reading like news:

- "Are you not hungry for breakfast in the morning? That's high cortisol."
- "They told you red meat and butter would clog your arteries."
- "Most people who die in their sleep never realize their body gives them three quiet warnings before it's too late."
- "This is the body of an average senior in 1940… And this is the body of an average senior today."
- "A single 300-page report published in 1910 that wiped out an entire branch of medicine in less than a year."
- "Ladies, did you know that if your farts stink really bad..."
- "Can I show you something terrifying?"
- "Meet the tribe that's baffled scientists for decades."
- "Are your shoes killing you?"
- "Why did heart disease rates suddenly explode in the 1940s?"
- "Inside: The 'banned' hospital department that kept 75-year-olds' hearts strong."
- "They call it the 'medical apocalypse' of the 1900's…"

Note the emotionally charged words: farts, baffled, terrifying, killing, explode, banned, medical apocalypse. These words do the heavy lifting.

## The generation loop

Prime on the brand's **hook primer** → mine seeds from the brief/winning ads → generate 10 hooks against the bar → **double-pass** (take the best, apply **Transfer / Reframe / Promote**, push to Level 3, generate 10 more) = 20 total → human selects → winners go back into the hook primer. That last step is the loopback: every selected hook makes the next run's primer stronger.
